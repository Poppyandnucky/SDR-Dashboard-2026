Model Structure

The model represents maternal care as a dynamic system connecting care-seeking behaviour, facility capacity, referral networks, clinical outcomes, and health system investments. It was developed to evaluate Service Delivery Redesign (SDR) strategies in Kakamega County and explore how interventions may affect maternal health over time.

The simulation tracks pregnant women from the point of labour through delivery and postpartum care. Women make decisions about where to deliver based on distance, facility characteristics, and exposure to community-level interventions. Health facilities have finite capacity and varying levels of readiness to manage complications.

Key Assumptions

Mothers choose where to deliver

Delivery decisions are influenced by distance, facility availability, referral pathways, intervention exposure, and facility characteristics.

Capacity matters

Facilities have finite capacity. As demand exceeds available resources, service quality and health outcomes may deteriorate.

Outcomes depend on access and readiness

Maternal outcomes are influenced by where care is received and whether facilities possess the staff, equipment, and resources needed to manage complications.

Interventions interact

The impact of individual interventions may depend on the presence of other interventions within the health system.

Data Used

County administrative data

Facility-level utilization records, birth volumes, and referral patterns from Kakamega County health facilities. These data inform baseline care-seeking patterns and facility capacity constraints.

Stakeholder consultations

Input from county health leadership and implementation partners helped validate model structure, intervention definitions, and plausible implementation scenarios.

Model calibration

Parameters were calibrated to match historical trends in facility delivery rates, maternal mortality ratios, and other observed outcomes from county surveillance systems.

Published Literature

Clinical effectiveness parameters are drawn from systematic reviews and meta-analyses of maternal health interventions. Key sources include:

  • Effect sizes for community mobilization, transport vouchers, and facility readiness improvements
  • Case fatality rates for obstetric complications by level of care and facility readiness
  • Care-seeking behaviour patterns from demographic and health surveys and qualitative studies in similar contexts

Story 03 From HSS

Where are women giving birth?

The strategy succeeds in shifting deliveries from home and small clinics (L2/3) to higher-level facilities (L4/5) — exactly where complications can be managed.

Why this matters: Most maternal complications are survivable at L4/5 but not at home or L2/3. Shifting delivery location is the single highest-leverage outcome of HSS.
